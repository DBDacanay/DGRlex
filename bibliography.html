Sources used

The Tłı̨chǫ Online Dictionary draws entries from many different sources. The source for each entry is written below the headword.

Most of the entries in the online dictionary are taken from existing written sources, which are listed by shorthand (see list below). However, a large number of entries have also been provided for the dictionary by fluent speakers. In these cases, the source is listed as the name of the fluent speaker, followed by the year (in brackets) when they provided the word (e.g. Rosa Mantla (2024)).

The major written sources used in the dictionary are listed below, in rough order of the number of entries provided by each:

Dogrib Dictionary (1996)

Saxon, L., Siemens, M. (1996). Tłı̨chǫ Yatıı̀ Enı̨htł’è - Dogrib Dictionary. Dogrib Divisional Board of Education.

Supplement to the Dogrib Dictionary (1999)

White, M. L., Siemens, M., Zoe, S., Mackenzie, E., Erasmus, J., Beaverhoe, J., Monus, V., Feenstra, J. (1999). Tłı̨chǫ Yatıı̀ Enı̨htł'è gha yatı nàts'ehtsı̨̀ - Supplement to the Dogrib Dictionary. Dogrib Community Services Board.

Dogrib New Testament (2008)

Canadian Bible Society (2003). Nǫ̀htsı̨ Nı̨htł'è: Zezı̀ wegǫ̀hłı̨ tł'axǫǫ̀ - Dogrib New Testament. Lac La Ronge Indian Band.

Dogrib Field Notes (1967)

Howren, R. (1967). Dogrib Fieldnotes - 1967 Book II. July-August 1967. Robert Howren Fonds (N-2006-003-1-1). NWT Archives, Prince of Wales Northern Heritage Centre, Yellowknife, Northwest Territories.

Common Fish in the Tłı̨chǫ Region (2003)

Clark, K., Vecsei, P. (2003). Common Fish in the Tłı̨chǫ Region. Wek'̀eezhı̀ı Renewable Resources Board.

Dogrib Traditional Place Names (2014)

Legat, A., Chocolate, G., Chocolate, M., Zoe, S. A. (2014). Habitat of Dogrib Traditional Territory: Place Names as Indicators of Biogeographical Knowledge. Tłı̨chǫ Government.

Tłı̨chǫ Birthplace Mapping Project (2016)

Olson, R., Kuntz, J. (2016). Edı̨ı̨̀ Gots'ǫ Gogǫ̀hłı̨: Tłı̨chǫ Birthplace Mapping Project. Tłı̨chǫ Government.

Elders' Medicinal Knowledge Database (2024)

Rabesca, M. A., Romie, D., Ryan, J., Johnson, M. (2024). Whatı̀got'ı̨ı̨̀ Medicine Tłı̨chǫ Dene Elders' Knowledge Databases. Tłı̨chǫ Government.

Knowledge of Ɂewaànıt'ııtı̀ (2013)

Tsatchia, M., Michel, E., Mantla, R., Mackenzie, R., Tlokka, P., Apples, H., Judas, J., Kodzin, J., Dryneck, P., Wetrade, R., Kodzin, N., Jacobsen, P. (2013). Tłı̨chǫ Use and Knowledge of Ɂewaànıt'ııtı̀. Tłı̨chǫ Government.

In addition to these major sources, a number of other, smaller sources were also consulted for further information on individual entries. These sources are listed below in alphabetical order, without shorthand, and are cited in the relevant entries in the APA style:

Chocolate, G., Birlea, M., Jacobsen, P. (2015). K'ıchıı̀ (Whitebeach Point) Traditional Knowledge Study. Tłı̨chǫ Government.

Chocolate, G., van der Vielen, S., Jacobsen, P. (2015). K'àgòò Tı̨lıı̀ Deè: Traditional Knowledge Study for the Proposed All-Season Road to Whatì. Tłı̨chǫ Research and Training Institute.

Hoijer, H. (1963). Studies in the Athabaskan Languages. Summer Institute of Linguistics (SIL) International (N-2006-013: 3-5). NWT Archives, Prince of Wales Northern Heritage Centre, Yellowknife, Northwest Territories.

Mantla, R. (2017). Mǫ̀ht'a Gòehk'ǫ (I Made a Fire): Tłı̨chǫ Worldview, the Role of Language in Tłı̨chǫ Puberty Camp. [Master's Thesis, University of Victoria]. University of Victoria Libraries.

Pasquayak, M., Klein, H. (2023). Sǫǫ̀mbakweè K'egoɂǫ̀ (Where There's Mineral Wealth): The Experiences of the Tłı̨chǫ with Giant Mine. Tłı̨chǫ Government.

Zoe, J. (2007). Trails of Our Ancestors: Building a Nation. Tłı̨chǫ Government.
