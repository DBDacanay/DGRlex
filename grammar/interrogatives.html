Questions

As in English, there are two basic types of questions in Tłı̨chǫ; closed questions and open questions. Closed questions, or ‘yes-or-no’ questions, are questions with a limited range of possible answers (generally hęɂę (‘yes’) or ı̨̀le (‘no’)):

Bò ghaı̨wı̨ nı̀?

Are you hungry?

Ası̨̀ı̨̀ Ilabè wezha anet’e?

Are you Elizabeth’s son?

There are several ways to form closed questions in Tłı̨chǫ. One of the most common is to use the particle nı̀, which is placed at the very end of a sentence, and converts it into a closed question:

Nànetso.

You are strong.

Nànetso nı̀?

Are you strong?

Another way of forming closed questions is with the particle ası̨̀ı̨̀, which is placed at the very beginning of the sentence:

Wı̨ı̨zı̀ k’èhǫǫzǫ.

You know his name.

Ası̨̀ı̨̀ wı̨ı̨zı̀ k’èhǫǫzǫ?

Do you know his name?

Ası̨̀ı̨̀ and nı̀ can also be used together on the same sentence, which accomplishes the same effect:

Ası̨̀ı̨̀ wı̨ı̨zı̀ k’èhǫǫzǫ nı̀?

Do you know his name?

Closed questions can also be formed by simply changing the intonation of a typical, non-question sentence. For example:

Wı̨ı̨zı̀ k’èhǫǫzǫ?

Do you know his name?

As mentioned, closed questions can generally be answered by either ‘yes’ (hęɂę) or ‘no’ (ı̨̀le). However, there are typically a small number of other possible responses to closed questions. One of the most common is dàodı̀. Literally, dàodı̀ means ‘nothing’; however, when used as a response to a closed question, it takes on an emphatically negative meaning (something like the English ‘not at all’, or ‘none whatsoever’):

Kwet’ı̨ı̨̀ k’èè gode nı̀?

Does she speak English?

Dàodı̀.

Not at all.

Sǫǫ̀mba nets’ǫ nı̀?

Do you have any money?

Dàodı̀.

No, none.

Closed questions can also be answered with sǫnı or sǫnıa, an interjection which indicates that the answer is either not known or is unclear (something like the English ‘I don’t know’ or ‘Maybe’):

Kwet’ı̨ı̨̀ k’èè gode nı̀?

Does she speak English?

Sǫnı.

Possibly (but I don’t know).

Open questions are questions which cannot be answered with a simple ‘yes’ or ‘no’, and require a more detailed or specific response:

Amıı̀ nèot’ı̨ı̨̀?

Who is your family?

Lıbò welaedı̀?

Where is the cup?

Ayı̀ı ghǫ sèahtı̨?

What are you two eating?

There are many different ‘interrogatives’ (or, question words) used to form open questions. These include:

ayı̀ı

what

amıı̀

who

ayı̀ı gha

why, how come

ayı̀ı ghǫ

why

dàanı̀ gha

why

dàanı̀ ghǫ

why

dànııt'à

why

ayı̀ı t'à

why

dàanı̀

how

dàht'e

when

dàtłǫ

how many
